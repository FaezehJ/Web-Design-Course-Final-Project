		    <li class="btmspace-15"><a href="http://infocus.nlm.nih.gov/2014/12/31/the-visible-human-project-at-20/"><em class="heading">Featured Article</em></a></li>
			  <h3>The Visible Human Project at 20 (Posted by NLM in Focus / December 31, 2014)</h3>
			  <li class="btmspace-15"><a href="http://infocus.nlm.nih.gov/2014/12/31/the-visible-human-project-at-20/"><em class="heading"></em><img class="borderedbox" src="images/demo/humananatomy.gif" alt=""></a></li>
			  <p>This cross-section view shows the Visible Human Male (left), who debuted in 1994, and the Female
			    (right), released in 1995. If something has been used regularly for 20 years, you might expect it
				to show signs of aging. Not so for the Visible Human Project. The National Library of Medicine (NLM)
				introduced the<a href="http://infocus.nlm.nih.gov/2014/12/31/the-visible-human-project-at-20/">Visible Human Project (VHP)</a>
				in November 1994 and in doing so revolutionized our ability to view and understand human anatomy.
				NLM’s Michael Ackerman, PhD, a biomedical engineer, came up with the idea for the visible humans in the 
				1980s, at a time when the library’s long range plan included a recommendation to investigate the feasibility
				of a biomedical images library. The VHP is a library of digital images representing the complete anatomy of 
				a man and a woman. NLM funded researchers at the University of Colorado Health Sciences Center to create the 
				images.Two cadavers were imaged from head to toe using computed tomography, magnetic resonance, and x-rays.
				Then, the cadavers were frozen, milled a thin layer at a time, and digitally photographed. Thousands of people
				worldwide have licensed the image dataset, free of charge, to study or create all kinds of things—artwork, 
				prosthetics, educational materials, and surgical simulators, for example.</p>

					<h3>Protect Your Heart</h3>
					<p>Although many people with type 2 diabetes worry about losing their vision or having an amputation, 
			           the greater risk is to the heart and brain. About 65% of people with type 2 diabetes die of heart 
				       disease or stroke. They are two to four times more likely to die of heart disease than people without
				       diabetes. "When someone does get a diagnosis of diabetes, they probably have had prediabetes for as long
				       as 10 years," says Gerald Bernstein, MD. "By the time their diagnosis is made, their risk for cardiovascular 
				       disease is extremely high. And then 10 years later, they will have their first cardiovascular event."</p>
